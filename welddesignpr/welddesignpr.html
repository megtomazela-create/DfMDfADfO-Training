The Weld Workshop

Master the principles of nuclear weld design

Welcome to the workshop, Engineer.

In nuclear, welding isn't just joining metal—it's one of the most heavily scrutinised activities in your entire project.

Every weld you design will require qualified procedures, certified welders, multiple inspections, and documentation that follows the component for 60 years of plant life.

Get it right in design, and manufacturing flows smoothly. Get it wrong, and you'll face costly redesigns, schedule delays, and difficult conversations with your Design Authority.

Let's learn the principles that will make you a better weld designer.

Why Every Weld Carries Significant Cost

It's not the welding—it's everything around it

In conventional fabrication, a weld might cost a few hours of labour. In nuclear, that same weld triggers a cascade of requirements that can multiply the true cost by 10x or more.

1 Procedure Qualification
Before anyone welds, the procedure itself must be qualified. A Welding Procedure Specification (WPS) defines exactly how the weld will be made. The Procedure Qualification Record (PQR) proves the procedure produces acceptable results.
  • Requires test pieces and destructive testing
  • Third-party review required
  • Under ASME Section IX and RCC-M Section IV, every variable matters
  • You may need dozens of qualified procedures
2 Welder Qualification
Only qualified welders can perform nuclear welds. Each welder must demonstrate competence for specific procedures, positions, and materials.
  • Qualifications must be maintained through continuous work
  • Re-testing required if qualification lapses
  • Records must be kept and available for audit
3 Pre-Weld Requirements
  • Fit-up inspection and approval before welding begins
  • Material verification (correct heat numbers, traceability)
  • Joint preparation check (geometry, cleanliness)
  • Preheat verification where required
  • All documented with hold points
4 In-Process Controls
  • Interpass temperature monitoring
  • Weld sequence control
  • Witness hold points for critical welds
  • Real-time documentation of parameters
5 Post-Weld Inspection
  • Visual Testing (VT) - 100% of all welds
  • Surface examination: PT or MT
  • Volumetric examination: RT or UT
  • For Class 1 components, expect multiple NDT methods
  • Each method requires qualified procedures and certified inspectors
6 Post-Weld Heat Treatment
Where required:
  • Controlled heating and cooling cycles
  • Thermocouple placement and monitoring
  • Documentation of time-temperature profiles
  • Often witnessed by third party
7 Documentation Package
Every weld gets a complete record package:
  • WPS used, welder ID, materials, consumables
  • All inspection results and any repairs
  • Package lives with the component forever
  • Required for in-service inspection planning

This is why weld design matters. Every weld you add to your design triggers this entire cascade. Reduce welds, and you reduce cost and schedule proportionally.

Principle 1: Minimise Welds

The best weld is the one you don't need

Every weld you eliminate removes:

  • A qualified procedure requirement
  • Welder time and qualification overhead
  • Multiple inspection activities
  • A potential defect location
  • Documentation burden
  • An in-service inspection point for 60 years

Techniques to reduce weld count

  • 1 Combine parts through design Instead of welding multiple plates together, can you specify a single larger plate?
  • 2 Use forgings or formed components A forged nozzle-to-shell connection eliminates welds. Yes, forgings cost more upfront—but total through-life cost may be lower.
  • 3 Challenge every weld in design review For each weld, ask: "Why is this joint here? Can we eliminate it?"
  • 4 Consider modular assembly If you must have welds, can they be made in controlled factory conditions?

Principle 2: Design for Welder Access

If the welder can't reach it properly, quality suffers

A weld can only be as good as the conditions allow. Confined spaces, awkward positions, and restricted visibility all degrade weld quality and increase the risk of defects.

Poor Access

  • Welder must work overhead
  • Limited torch angle
  • Restricted visibility
  • Ventilation difficult
  • Higher defect probability

Good Access

  • Flat or horizontal position
  • Full torch manipulation
  • Clear visibility
  • Good ventilation
  • Faster, higher quality, fewer repairs

What welders need

  • Physical space Room for the welding torch, correct torch angle (typically 15-45° from perpendicular), clearance for equipment
  • Body position Welders produce best quality in comfortable, stable positions. Overhead and vertical-up are more difficult than flat.
  • Visibility The welder must see the weld pool clearly. Restricted sight lines lead to defects.
  • Ventilation Welding produces fumes that must be extracted. Confined spaces require mechanical ventilation.

Designer Checklist

Can a welder physically reach this joint?
What position will they weld in? (Flat is best)
Is there room for torch manipulation?
Can they see the weld pool?
How will fumes be extracted?
Have you consulted manufacturing early?

Talk to your manufacturing team during design. They can tell you in minutes what will take months to discover if you wait until fabrication.

Principle 3: Design for Inspector Access

If you can't inspect it, you can't use it

Every nuclear weld WILL be inspected—during manufacture and throughout the plant's 60-year operating life. In-service inspection (ISI) requirements under ASME Section XI or RSE-M mean your weld must remain accessible and inspectable for decades.

Poor Inspection Access

  • UT scanning surface blocked
  • RT single-wall access only
  • Limited probe manipulation
  • ISI will be problematic

Good Inspection Access

  • Full UT scanning surface
  • RT access both sides
  • Clear for VT and PT
  • ISI provisions included

NDT Methods & Access Requirements

Visual Testing (VT)

Direct line of sight to weld surface. Adequate lighting (min 500 lux). Both sides where possible.

Penetrant Testing (PT)

Direct surface access for penetrant application, cleaning, and developer. Temperature control (10-52°C).

Magnetic Particle (MT)

Access to apply magnetic field. Surface access for particles. Ferromagnetic materials only.

Ultrasonic Testing (UT)

Smooth scanning surface adjacent to weld. 100-150mm clearance either side. Access from both sides preferred.

Radiographic Testing (RT)

Access to BOTH sides—source one side, film other. Safety exclusion zone required during exposure.

Designer Checklist

Can VT access this weld from both sides?
Is there scanning surface for UT probes?
Can RT source and film be positioned correctly?
Will this access still exist after installation?
What about during plant operation?
Have you consulted your ISI team?

The AFCEN inspectability guide states: the inspectability analysis must be positioned in the overall design process, particularly during the preparation of the hazard analysis. Don't leave this until manufacturing.

Key Takeaways

Every Weld Triggers a Cascade

In nuclear, every weld requires procedure qualification, welder qualification, multiple inspections, and documentation. The true cost is many times the physical welding.

Minimise Welds

The best weld is the one you don't need. Challenge every weld joint in your design. Combine parts, use forgings, eliminate unnecessary joints.

Design for Welder Access

Welders need physical space, comfortable positions, visibility, and ventilation. Poor access means poor quality and repairs. Consult manufacturing early.

Design for Inspector Access

Every weld will be inspected during manufacture AND for 60 years of plant life. Different NDT methods need different access. If you can't inspect it, you can't use it.

Before you finalise any weld, ask yourself:

  1. Is this weld necessary?
  2. Can the welder reach it and produce quality work?
  3. Can inspectors examine it using required NDT methods?
  4. Will it still be inspectable after 30 years of operation?

If you can't answer yes to all four, redesign before you build.
