Material Selection Constraints

Why you can't just specify any material in nuclear

In other industries, you might specify a material grade and source it from any qualified supplier within weeks. In nuclear, material selection is fundamentally different.

You must use code-approved materials. Your choices are limited. Lead times are measured in months, not weeks. And that 'equivalent' material your supplier is offering? It won't work here.

This module explains why—and what it means for your design decisions.

Carbon & Low-Alloy Steels

6-12 months typical

Approved Grades (Examples)

SA-516 Gr.70 SA-508 Gr.3 16MND5 SA-533 Type B P355

Typical Applications

  • Reactor pressure vessel
  • Steam generators (shell side)
  • Pressuriser
  • Primary circuit piping
  • Structural supports

Key Constraints

  • Must meet specific impact toughness requirements (Charpy testing)
  • Elevated temperature tensile testing required for each heat
  • Vacuum treatment mandatory for Class 1 components
  • Maximum carbon content limits for weldability
  • Full material traceability from melt to final product

Austenitic Stainless Steels

4-8 months typical

Approved Grades (Examples)

SA-240 304/304L SA-240 316/316L SA-312 304/316 SA-182 F304/F316 Z2CN18-10

Typical Applications

  • Reactor internals
  • Primary circuit cladding
  • Spent fuel pool liners
  • Auxiliary system piping
  • Control rod guide tubes

Key Constraints

  • Delta ferrite content controlled (RCC-M: 5-15%, ASME: 5FN minimum)
  • Sensitisation testing for specific applications
  • Intergranular corrosion resistance verification
  • Low carbon grades (L) required for welded applications
  • Solution annealing heat treatment requirements

Nickel-Based Alloys

8-18 months typical

Approved Grades (Examples)

Alloy 600 Alloy 690 Alloy 625 Alloy 718 Alloy 800

Typical Applications

  • Steam generator tubes (Alloy 690)
  • Reactor vessel head penetrations
  • Control rod drive mechanism housings
  • Pressuriser heater sleeves
  • High-temperature components

Key Constraints

  • Low cobalt content required (reduces activation)
  • Stress corrosion cracking resistance critical
  • Specific heat treatment sequences mandatory
  • Very limited approved suppliers globally
  • Extensive qualification testing required

Key Point

For most structural and pressure boundary applications in SMR, you'll be working with carbon steels, stainless steels, and nickel alloys. Speciality materials are used for specific components and require even longer lead times and more limited supplier options.

Lead Time Reality Check

Why nuclear materials take months, not weeks

Conventional Industry

W1
Specify material grade
W2
Get quotes from suppliers
W3
Place order
W4
Material delivered
TOTAL: 2-4 weeks

Nuclear Industry

M1-2
Specify material to code requirements, identify approved suppliers
M3
Supplier audits and qualification verification
M4-5
Order placed, material manufactured with witness hold points
M6-8
Testing (mechanical, chemical, NDT) with third-party witness
M9-10
Documentation review, certification package assembly
M11-12
Delivery with full traceability package
TOTAL: 6-12 months (or longer)

Why the difference?

Limited Approved Suppliers

Not every steel mill can produce nuclear-grade material. Suppliers must be qualified, audited, and maintain nuclear quality programmes.

Witness Hold Points

Critical manufacturing and testing stages require independent witness—often a third-party inspector must be present.

Enhanced Testing

Every heat requires full mechanical testing, chemical analysis, and often NDT. Tests must be witnessed and certified.

Documentation Package

A nuclear material certificate isn't a single page. It's a complete package: heat certs, test reports, traceability records.

No Substitutions

If a test fails or material is rejected, you can't just grab equivalent stock. You start again with a new heat.

Designer Impact: Your material choices must be finalised early. Late changes to material specifications can add 6-12 months to your programme. Design with procurement lead times in mind.

The "Equivalent" Trap

Why equivalent materials don't work in nuclear

You've specified SA-516 Grade 70 for a pressure vessel component. Your procurement team comes back with good news: a supplier has offered 'SA-516 Grade 70 equivalent' at 40% less cost and 8 weeks faster delivery.

Do you accept?

REJECTED — Here's why this fails:

No Code Stamp

'Equivalent' materials are not manufactured under a nuclear quality programme. They don't carry the code certification (ASME N-stamp or RCC-M equivalent) required for nuclear pressure boundary applications.

Incomplete Traceability

Nuclear materials require complete traceability from raw material through every manufacturing step. Equivalent materials typically have commercial-grade documentation—heat numbers and basic test certs—but not the witnessed, verified chain required for nuclear.

Testing Gaps

The enhanced testing required for nuclear (specific impact testing, elevated temperature tensiles, witnessed NDT) hasn't been performed. The material properties are assumed, not proven.

No Regulatory Acceptance

When ONR or your Independent Nuclear Safety Assessment team reviews the safety case, 'equivalent' material will not be accepted. You'll have to remove it and start again.

Supply Chain Integrity

Using non-code materials opens the door to counterfeit and fraudulent items. The nuclear industry has strict controls precisely because the consequences of material failure are severe.

CORRECT — Good decision.

In nuclear, 'equivalent' is not acceptable for safety-classified components. The material must be:

Manufactured under a nuclear quality programme

Fully traceable from melt to delivery

Tested to code requirements with independent witness

Certified with complete documentation package

From an approved/audited supplier

The 40% cost saving would become a 400% cost overrun when you have to rip it out and replace it. Plus months of schedule delay. Plus regulatory scrutiny of your entire supply chain.

Designer Impact: When you specify a material, you're not just picking a grade—you're committing to the nuclear supply chain. Specify code-approved materials from the start. Don't create opportunities for procurement to find 'equivalent' shortcuts.

Key Takeaways

Code-Approved Only

You must specify materials approved by RCC-M or ASME Section III. Your choices are limited compared to other industries—work within these constraints from the start.

Plan for Lead Times

Nuclear materials take 6-12 months (sometimes longer). Finalise your material specifications early. Late changes can devastate your programme schedule.

No Equivalents

'Equivalent' materials are not acceptable for safety-classified components. The nuclear supply chain exists for a reason—don't try to shortcut it.

Traceability is Everything

Every piece of nuclear material must be fully traceable from melt to installation. Your material choice locks in documentation requirements for the entire supply chain.

Material selection in nuclear isn't just about mechanical properties—it's about the entire qualified supply chain that delivers assured quality to your component.
