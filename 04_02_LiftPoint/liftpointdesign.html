SECTION A

Why Every Lift Needs a Plan

LOLER 1998 Regulation 8 is absolute: all lifting operations must be properly planned by a competent person, appropriately supervised, and carried out safely.

The Appointed Person

Under BS 7121, the competent person responsible for planning lifting operations is called the Appointed Person. This is not a casual title — it carries legal weight.

The Appointed Person must:

  • Plan the lifting operation
  • Select appropriate equipment and accessories
  • Select competent persons for each role
  • Ensure all equipment is certified and examined
  • Produce the method of work
LOLER 1998 — Regulation 8(1)

"Every employer shall ensure that every lifting operation involving lifting equipment is properly planned by a competent person, appropriately supervised and carried out in a safe manner."

Planning Is Not Optional

A lifting operation is defined as "an operation concerned with the lifting or lowering of a load" (Regulation 8(2)). This includes:

  • Every crane lift on site
  • Module installation operations
  • Equipment moves during outages
  • Even simple hoist operations

No lift is too small to plan. The regulation applies regardless of load weight.

Lifting accessories (slings, shackles, chains) require thorough examination every 6 months under LOLER Regulation 9.

SECTION B

What Goes Wrong

When lifting operations fail, people die. These are not hypotheticals — they are prosecuted cases with real victims and real consequences.

Brand Energy — £1.6 million fine (2024)

24-year-old Jack Phillips was crushed and killed when a lifting sling failed, dropping the load onto him.

Failures identified:

  • No proper planning of the lifting operation
  • No exclusion zones established
  • Lifting accessories (slings) not properly inspected
  • Out-of-date slings used

"This tragic death could have been prevented if the employer had fulfilled its statutory duty to plan and manage the risks associated with lifting operations."

— HSE Principal Inspector Ross Carter
Balfour Beatty — £285,000 fine (2024)

A crane pushed over a scissor lift during cladding installation. One worker (Igor Malka) fell 10 metres to his death. A second worker sustained spinal injuries, broken ribs, and required pins in his pelvis and thigh.

Failures identified:

  • No lift supervisor present
  • No communication between crane team and cladding team
  • Principal contractor failed to coordinate activities

Regulation breached: LOLER Regulation 8(1)

Aztech BA — Suspended prison sentence + £100,000 fine

Worker Ian Middlemiss was crushed and killed by a 2-tonne metal moulding box that fell from an overhead crane.

Failures identified:

  • Crane had not been tested or examined as required by law
  • No competency assessment, supervision, or training
  • Company directors knew improvements were needed but did not act

These are not freak accidents. Every case involved failures that were known, preventable, and prosecuted. The HSE will investigate. Companies will be fined. Directors can face prison.

SECTION C

Your Duties as a Designer

Designers are not exempt from lifting safety. CDM 2015 Regulation 9 places direct duties on anyone who prepares or modifies a design.

Designers Can Be Prosecuted

CDM 2015 Regulation 9 requires designers to:

  • Eliminate foreseeable risks so far as reasonably practicable
  • If elimination is not possible, reduce or control the risks through design
  • Provide information about significant residual risks

This applies to:

  • Lift point locations and design
  • Module weight and centre of gravity calculations
  • Rigging interface specifications
  • Access for lifting operations
Case Reference

In a 2017 prosecution, an architectural practice was fined alongside a construction company after a worker fell to his death. The HSE inspector stated: "While it is rare for designers to be charged with breaching health and safety legislation, they must be aware they can be held responsible where bad design is an important contributory factor to a workplace fatality."

The ERIC Framework

E

Eliminate

Remove the hazard entirely through design

Example: Design module to be lifted from ground level
R

Reduce

Minimise risk through design choices

Example: Position lift points to keep CoG central
I

Inform

Communicate residual risks to others

Example: Provide CoG data in design documentation
C

Control

Design in control measures

Example: Include permanent lifting lugs

What Designers Must Provide

For any component that will be lifted, the designer must provide:

  • Verified weight (not estimated)
  • Centre of gravity location (calculated, not assumed central)
  • Lift point specifications — locations, capacities, orientation requirements
  • Rigging constraints — minimum sling angles, prohibited attachment points
  • Residual risk information — anything unusual about the lift
CDM 2015 — Regulation 9(4)

"A designer must take all reasonable steps to provide, with the design, sufficient information about the design, construction and maintenance of the structure to adequately assist the client, other designers and contractors to comply with their duties."

SECTION D

Nuclear Lifting Requirements

Nuclear facilities have additional requirements beyond standard construction. Single-failure-proof cranes, seismic qualification, and heavy load handling programmes are mandatory.

The Polar Crane

Polar cranes are the primary heavy lift equipment inside reactor containment buildings. They operate on circular rails near the dome, enabling 360° rotation to reach any point in the reactor hall.

Single-Failure-Proof Requirements

Under ASME NOG-1, cranes handling critical loads in nuclear facilities must be single-failure-proof (Type I). This means:

  • Any single failure in the hoisting system must not result in dropping the load
  • Redundant braking systems
  • Redundant load paths
  • Enhanced inspection and testing requirements

NUREG-0612 governs heavy load handling at nuclear facilities, requiring:

  • Defined safe load paths
  • Minimum elevation during lifts
  • Exclusion of non-essential personnel
  • Heavy load handling procedures

In a nuclear facility, a dropped load is not just a safety incident — it is a potential nuclear safety event. The consequences of damaging reactor components, fuel assemblies, or safety systems are catastrophic.
